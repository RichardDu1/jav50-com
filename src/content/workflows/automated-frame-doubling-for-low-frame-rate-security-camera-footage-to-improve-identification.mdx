## Automated Frame Doubling for Low-Frame-Rate Security Camera Footage

### Overview
Security cameras often record at low frame rates (e.g., 5-15 FPS) to save storage, but this makes identifying fast-moving objects difficult. This workflow uses AI frame interpolation to double the frame rate, creating smoother motion and clearer stills for identification.

### Prerequisites
- **Hardware**: GPU with at least 4GB VRAM (NVIDIA GTX 1060 or better).
- **Software**: 
  - [DaVinci Resolve Studio](https://www.blackmagicdesign.com/products/davinciresolve) (paid, $295 one-time) or free version with limited features.
  - [Topaz Video AI](https://www.topazlabs.com/topaz-video-ai) (paid, $299 one-time or subscription).
  - [FFmpeg](https://ffmpeg.org/) (free, open-source).
- **Footage**: Low-frame-rate video file (e.g., 10 FPS, MP4 format).

### Step 1: Prepare Your Footage
1. Transfer the security camera clip to your workstation.
2. Use FFmpeg to extract metadata and confirm frame rate:
   ```bash
   ffmpeg -i input.mp4
   ```
   Look for `Stream #0:0 Video: ... 10 fps`.

### Step 2: Frame Interpolation with Topaz Video AI
1. Open Topaz Video AI.
2. Drag and drop your video file.
3. Under **AI Model**, select **Chronos** (best for motion interpolation).
4. Set **Output Frame Rate** to double the original (e.g., 20 FPS if original is 10 FPS).
5. Enable **Motion Deblur** if footage is blurry.
6. Click **Start** to process. This may take 10-30 minutes depending on GPU.
7. Export as **ProRes 422** or **H.264** for further editing.

### Step 3: Enhance with DaVinci Resolve Studio (Optional)
1. Import the interpolated video into DaVinci Resolve.
2. Go to the **Color** page and apply **Noise Reduction** (use Temporal NR for low-light footage).
3. Use **Super Scale** (in Clip Attributes) to upscale resolution if needed (e.g., 720p to 1080p).
4. Export final video as **H.264** with high bitrate (20 Mbps).

### Step 4: Automate with FFmpeg (Advanced)
For batch processing, create a script:
```bash
for f in *.mp4; do
  ffmpeg -i "$f" -filter:v "minterpolate=fps=30:mi_mode=mci:mc_mode=aobmc:me_mode=bidir:vsbmc=1" -c:v libx264 -crf 18 "${f%.*}_doubled.mp4"
done
```
This uses FFmpeg's built-in motion interpolation (slower but free).

### Tips for Best Results
- Use footage with minimal compression artifacts.
- For identification, extract still frames from the interpolated video using:
  ```bash
  ffmpeg -i output.mp4 -vf "select='not(mod(n,30))'" -vsync vfr frames_%04d.png
  ```
- Store original and processed footage separately for audit trails.

### Troubleshooting
- **Artifacts**: If interpolation creates ghosting, try a lower frame rate multiplier (e.g., 1.5x instead of 2x).
- **GPU Out of Memory**: Reduce resolution or use CPU mode (slower).

### Conclusion
Automated frame doubling significantly improves the usability of low-FPS security footage. With Topaz Video AI, you can achieve professional results in minutes, while FFmpeg offers a free alternative for batch processing.
